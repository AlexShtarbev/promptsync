# Story Candidates — Ranked by Pre-Mortem Survival Rate

## Tier 1 — Highest Viral Survival Rate

### Cassandra

**What everyone thinks:** She could see the future but nobody believed her. Sad.

**What they forgot:** What happens to a person's mind when they're always right and always dismissed? It's not a prophecy story. It's a gaslighting story. She's the first person in literature to be told her reality isn't real.

**Pre-mortem cause of death:** It becomes a "believe women" PSA. Heavy-handed, preachy, the audience feels lectured about a social issue instead of experiencing a myth. The modern resonance is so obvious that leaning into it makes it feel like an op-ed with pretty pictures.

**How it survives:** Stay inside the myth. A woman speaks. People turn away. Behind them, fire. Never say the modern word. Never make the connection explicit. The audience will write it in the comments. The video shows the myth. The audience does the political work. You don't editorialize — you trust the story.

| Criterion | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Instant recognition | Pass | Everyone knows the name |
| Strong reversal | Pass | Prophecy story → gaslighting story |
| AI-friendly | Pass | Single figure, reaction shots, fire as environment |
| Visual transformation | Pass | The fire proving her right while people walk away |
| Universal mirror | 10/10 | Everyone has been the person who was right and ignored |
| Saturation | Low | Not widely explored in short-form |
| Comment fuel | Extreme | Every viewer projects a different modern parallel |

### Sisyphus

**What everyone thinks:** Man pushes rock, rock rolls back, eternal suffering.

**What they forgot:** The punishment only works if the prisoner resists it. The moment Sisyphus stops seeing the boulder as punishment, the gods lose. It's not a suffering story. It's a story about a man who made the gods' weapon meaningless by choosing it.

**Pre-mortem cause of death:** Beautiful Nothing (gorgeous mountain, no value turn) or The Bumper Sticker (ending on "One must imagine Sisyphus happy").

**How it survives:** Quiet defiance — not rage, not collapse. The audience expects him to break when the boulder rolls back. He doesn't. He just turns around and walks back down. The defiance is in the absence of collapse. Open on hands, strain, stone — physical and urgent, not scenic. Let the audience recognize Sisyphus instantly, think they know the ending, and be wrong.

| Criterion | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Instant recognition | Pass | The boulder is universal |
| Strong reversal | Pass | Suffering → chosen defiance |
| AI-friendly | Pass | Silhouette, close-ups, aftermath shots — route around hand-on-rock |
| Visual transformation | Pass | Body language shift from broken to deliberate |
| Universal mirror | 9/10 | Everyone has a boulder |
| Saturation | Medium | Camus quotes are everywhere, but animated versions aren't |
| Comment fuel | Strong | "Is this hopeful or devastating?" |

**Creative direction locked:** Tone is quiet defiance. Audience recognizes Sisyphus from frame one but arrives with the wrong expectation. The reversal is visual — body, light, rhythm — never verbal.

### Orpheus and Eurydice

**What everyone thinks:** He loved her so much he went to the underworld, but he couldn't resist looking back. A love story about weakness.

**What they forgot:** Why did he look? Not curiosity. Not impatience. The darkest reading: he couldn't bear the weight of being her only hope. The turn of his head isn't love — it's self-sabotage. He destroys the thing he wants most because the responsibility of saving it is unbearable.

**Pre-mortem cause of death:** The reversal is psychologically subtle. The audience sees "man looked back, lost wife, sad" — the surface reading they already have. The 60 seconds play as a pretty animation of a love story, and the deeper reading never lands.

**How it survives:** Don't show the love story. Show THE TURN. The entire 60 seconds builds to a 4-second moment where his head begins to move, and the audience feels what's happening inside that movement. Not love. Not curiosity. Something wrong. Something self-destructive. The piece isn't about the underworld — it's about the three inches his head travels.

| Criterion | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Instant recognition | Pass | The "don't look back" is iconic |
| Strong reversal | Pass | Love → self-sabotage |
| AI-friendly | Pass | Two silhouettes, darkness, a single movement |
| Visual transformation | Pass | But requires precision — the micro-moment must read |
| Universal mirror | 9/10 | Everyone has sabotaged the thing they wanted most |
| Saturation | Medium | Well-known myth but the self-sabotage angle isn't mainstream |
| Comment fuel | Strong | "He did it on purpose" vs. "No, he just loved her too much" |

### Judas

**What everyone thinks:** The traitor. The worst person in the story. Thirty silver coins.

**What they forgot:** In the Gnostic Gospel of Judas, Jesus asked him to do it. Judas was the only disciple faithful enough to carry the one task that would damn him forever. The traitor was the most obedient. He agreed to be hated for eternity so the prophecy could be fulfilled.

**Pre-mortem cause of death:** Religious backlash. The video gets reported, restricted, or the comment section becomes a theological warzone that drives away casual viewers. The brand becomes "that controversial religion channel."

**How it survives:** Execute with reverence, not edge. This is real scholarship (Gnostic Gospel of Judas, 3rd-century codex). The tone is grief, not defiance. A man carrying a burden he chose. Silver coins as weight, not payment. If the execution feels sacred rather than edgy, the controversy is generative.

**Critical rule: this cannot be the pilot.** It needs 3-4 episodes of brand trust first. The audience needs to understand that Archives of the Forgotten approaches stories with care before you touch the most sensitive figure in Christianity.

| Criterion | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Instant recognition | Pass | Silver coins, the kiss, universal |
| Strong reversal | Explosive | Traitor → most faithful servant |
| AI-friendly | Pass | Single figure, darkness, coins, hands |
| Visual transformation | Pass | The weight of the coins, the solitude |
| Universal mirror | 9/10 | Anyone who did the necessary ugly thing |
| Saturation | Low | The Gnostic reading is not mainstream |
| Comment fuel | Nuclear | Thousands of comments guaranteed |

## Tier 2 — Strong, One Weakness to Manage

### Prometheus

**What everyone thinks:** He stole fire for humanity and was punished for it.

**What they forgot:** His name literally means "foresight." He knew the punishment before he acted. The fire wasn't impulse. The chains weren't surprise. He calculated the cost — eternity in chains against humanity freezing in the dark — and chose the chains. It's not a tragedy of consequence. It's a story about choosing suffering with full knowledge.

**Weakness:** Too similar to Sisyphus in surface reading. Both are "man endures eternal punishment by gods." Must be spaced 4-5 episodes apart. Sisyphus is about finding meaning in punishment. Prometheus is about choosing suffering for others. Sisyphus is internal. Prometheus is sacrificial. The distinction must be visible in the first 5 seconds.

| Criterion | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Instant recognition | Pass | Fire, chains, eagle |
| Strong reversal | Pass | Reckless theft → calculated sacrifice |
| AI-friendly | Pass | Chains, fire, eagle in separate shots |
| Visual transformation | Pass | The absence of surprise on his face when the eagle comes |
| Universal mirror | 8/10 | Anyone who made a sacrifice knowing the full cost |
| Saturation | Low | The foresight angle isn't widely explored |
| Comment fuel | Medium | Less debatable than Cassandra or Judas |

### The Minotaur

**What everyone thinks:** Monster in the labyrinth. Theseus is the hero who killed it.

**What they forgot:** The Minotaur was a child. Born from a curse on his mother, locked in a dark maze by his stepfather Minos, never asked to be what he was. Theseus is celebrated for killing what was essentially a prisoner in a cage. The "hero" murdered a boy who'd never seen sunlight.

**Weakness:** The audience may not have enough mythological context for the reversal. If you need exposition to set up the backstory, you burn 15-20 seconds of a 60-second piece.

**How to manage:** No backstory. Show a figure alone in darkness. Long shadows. The sound of breathing. Make the audience feel isolation before they know who they're watching. Then the "hero" arrives with a sword. The shadow on the wall looks monstrous — but the figure in the dark doesn't. The reversal is visual, not informational.

| Criterion | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Instant recognition | Pass | The labyrinth is iconic |
| Strong reversal | Pass | Monster → prisoner; hero → killer |
| AI-friendly | Pass | Labyrinth, darkness, single figure, shadow |
| Visual transformation | Pass | But requires the no-backstory visual approach |
| Universal mirror | 8/10 | Anyone who's been labeled the monster when they were the prisoner |
| Saturation | Low | The sympathetic Minotaur isn't mainstream |
| Comment fuel | Strong | "Theseus was the real monster" will spark debate |

### Pandora

**What everyone thinks:** She opened the box and released all evils into the world. Curiosity punished.

**What they forgot:** The last thing in the box was Hope. And it was locked in there with the evils. Why? Was Hope the antidote — meant to help humanity survive everything else? Or was Hope the cruelest evil of all — the thing that keeps you enduring suffering instead of escaping it?

**Weakness:** The question is philosophical, not visceral. Short-form rewards feeling, not thinking. Risk of "hmm, interesting" instead of an emotional response.

**How to manage:** Make Hope feel threatening, not comforting. Everything dark has escaped, the box is almost empty, and the last glow inside isn't warm — it's hungry. Hope isn't a gift. It's the thing that won't let you stop.

| Criterion | Score | Notes |
|-----------|-------|-------|
| Instant recognition | Pass | The box is iconic |
| Strong reversal | Pass | Gift → cruelest evil |
| AI-friendly | Pass | Box, light, darkness, single figure |
| Visual transformation | Medium | Abstract — needs Hope to feel physically threatening |
| Universal mirror | 7/10 | Anyone told to stay positive while drowning |
| Saturation | Low | The "Hope as evil" angle is rarely explored visually |
| Comment fuel | Strong | "Is Hope good or evil?" is a fight people will have |

## Stories That Seem Obvious but Fail

| Story | Why it seems right | Why it dies |
|-------|-------------------|-------------|
| **Icarus** | Iconic image — wings, sun, fall | No real reversal. "Don't fly too high" IS the meaning. Nothing is forgotten. The video illustrates what everyone already knows. |
| **Medusa** | Powerful reversal — she was a victim, not a monster | Already widely reclaimed. Feminist Medusa is on a thousand Instagram accounts. The "forgotten meaning" isn't forgotten anymore. |
| **Achilles** | He chose a short glorious life over a long quiet one | The Trojan War context is too complex for 60 seconds. 20 seconds of setup before you reach the choice. |
| **Persephone** | She may have chosen the underworld | Same saturation as Medusa. The feminist Persephone reading is already mainstream. |
| **Mary Magdalene** | Never called a prostitute in scripture | The reversal is a historical correction, not an emotional experience. "Huh, I didn't know that" isn't a share impulse. |
| **Tower of Babel** | God feared what humanity could build together | No protagonist. No face, no body, no transformation. Crowds and architecture are AI-animation nightmares. |
