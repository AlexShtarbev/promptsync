# Pre-Mortem Framework — How an Episode Dies

Six months from now, the video has 400 views. These are the causes of death.

---

## The Seven Kill Criteria

A story is evaluated against all seven. Failing any one is enough to kill the video.

| # | Criterion | What it means |
|---|-----------|---------------|
| 1 | **Instant recognition** | Audience places the myth in under 3 seconds |
| 2 | **Strong reversal** | A "forgotten meaning" that breaks their assumption |
| 3 | **AI-animation friendly** | No HIGH RISK physics, no crowds, no complex interaction |
| 4 | **Visual transformation** | The reversal is felt through body/light/environment, not explained |
| 5 | **Universal mirror** | Multiple audiences see themselves in it |
| 6 | **Low saturation** | Hasn't been done to death in this format |
| 7 | **Comment fuel** | The meaning is debatable, not obvious |

---

## Causes of Death (applicable to any episode)

### 1. Beautiful Nothing
Gorgeous AI mountainscapes, swelling music, and nothing happens. No value turn. No surprise. The myth feels so inherently profound that you assumed the profundity was self-evident. It wasn't. The audience saw a pretty animation, thought "cool," and scrolled. A mood is not a story.

### 2. The Bumper Sticker
You stated the theme out loud — in a text card, in VO, in a caption. The moment you said it, you killed the audience's chance to feel it themselves. Lectured viewers don't share.

### 3. Dead Hook
You opened with a wide establishing shot. Beautiful, slow, atmospheric. The viewer scrolled past in 1.4 seconds. A philosophical piece is MORE dependent on a savage hook, not less — because the audience's prior on "deep AI art" is that it's boring.

### 4. The AI Uncanny Valley
Complex motion — hands gripping objects, walking uphill, physics interactions — looked wrong. The hands melted. The gait was off. The audience couldn't feel moved because the figure didn't look like a person. Technical execution destroyed the emotional payload.

### 5. Repetition Without Escalation
The same visual or emotional beat repeated without anything changing. No escalation in stakes, no shift in the character's relationship to their situation, no visual progression. The middle sagged because nothing was evolving.

### 6. Preachy Tone
The modern parallel was made explicit. Text cards that connected the myth to current issues. VO that explained the lesson. The video felt like an op-ed with animation instead of a story that trusted its audience.

### 7. Generic AI Aesthetic
It looked like every other AI-animated piece — ethereal, over-lit, soft focus, cinematic color grading. The audience's brain categorized it as "AI art" in the first second and applied the "AI art" discount: interesting technically, emotionally disposable. No visual signature.

### 8. No Funnel
The video was a one-off. Even if it spiked, there was no series hook, no reason to follow, no next episode. A viral video without architecture is a firework — bright and gone.

### 9. The Reversal Didn't Land
The "forgotten meaning" was too subtle, too intellectual, or required context the audience didn't have. They watched the whole 60 seconds and understood only the surface reading they arrived with. The video confirmed their expectation instead of breaking it.

### 10. Wrong Controversy Timing
A religiously or culturally sensitive episode was released before the brand had trust. The audience didn't know "Archives of the Forgotten" approaches stories with care. The backlash defined the brand before the brand could define itself.
