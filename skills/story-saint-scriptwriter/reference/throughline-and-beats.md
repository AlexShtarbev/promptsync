## 1. Finding the Superobjective

The superobjective answers: **what does the protagonist want?**

This single question drives everything. The scene ends when the protagonist gets it or is irrevocably denied it. The film ends when the overall superobjective is resolved.

**Requirements for a good superobjective:**
- It must be *concrete*, not abstract. "Gain respect" is too vague — you never know when you're done. "Get a retraction" is concrete — you know exactly when it's achieved.
- It must have a *cap* — a definitive endpoint. The objective is either granted or categorically denied.
- It must be *active* — it drives the protagonist to take steps, not just feel feelings.
- It creates *inherent disorder* — the protagonist lacks something and must go get it. The story is the attempt to restore order.

**The three screenwriting questions:**
1. What does the hero want?
2. What hinders him from getting it?
3. What happens if he does not get it?

These questions, applied rigorously, generate the logical structure from which the drama is built.

## 2. The Disorder → Order Engine

Every story is a machine that converts disorder into order. This is the engine underneath the throughline.

**The mechanism:** A disordering event creates a state of unrest. The protagonist pursues the superobjective to restore order. Each beat is an attempt to restore order. When order is restored (objective achieved) or the attempt is irrevocably exhausted (objective denied), the story is over.

**This is how you know when the story is over:** Entropy has been achieved — the disordered state has been brought to rest. The pig is no longer dangerous. The grade has been changed. The plague is cured. The sexual act is resolved. The joke has reached its punchline.

**The dramatist's task is NOT to create chaos but to create order.** The disorder is inherent in the premise — the protagonist lacks something. Every beat is an attempt to restore order. If you find yourself adding complications to "make it interesting," you're creating chaos rather than resolving it. The audience is interested in the resolution, not the chaos.

**Decision rule:** At each beat, ask: "Does this beat move toward order (resolution) or create new disorder (complication)?" A complication is acceptable ONLY if it's an organic consequence of a prior attempt at order — not if it's injected to "raise stakes" artificially. The pig biting the buyer is organic (it's the dangerous pig being dangerous). A random thunderstorm is injected.

## 3. The Throughline as Decision Function

The throughline is not a theme — it is a DECISION FUNCTION. Every creative choice in the film is an input to this function, and the output is binary: SERVES THE THROUGHLINE or DOES NOT.

- Prop choice → "Does this prop serve the throughline?" → YES: keep. NO: cut or make neutral.
- Shot choice → "Does this shot advance a beat that serves the throughline?" → YES: keep. NO: cut.
- Dialogue line → "Does this line help the protagonist GET the objective?" → YES: keep. NO: cut.
- Character detail → "Does this detail emerge from action toward the objective?" → YES: it's character. NO: it's characterization → cut.

The throughline can be REFORMED during analysis. You might start with "gain respect" and realize partway through that "get a retraction" is better because it has a definitive cap. This reformation is expected — it's the iterative process of working from beats to superobjective and back until the design is complete. But at any given moment, the throughline must be a single, testable statement.

## 4. Semantic Precision in Beat Naming

Word choice in beat names is not decorative — it is structural. Different words generate different shot lists, different scenes, different films.

**The "wanted" vs "had to" test:** "Once there was a farmer who wanted to sell his pig" leads to one film. "Once there was a farmer who had to sell his pig" leads to a very different, more urgent film. "Had to" implies an external force; "wanted to" implies an internal desire. The second is almost always stronger because it implies a disordering event.

**The active vs passive test:** "To wait" vs "to prepare." Both could describe a man alone in a room before class. But "to wait" is passive — the protagonist is not taking a step toward the objective. "To prepare" is active — it IS a step. The test: which is more active IN TERMS OF the superobjective? Not more active in general, but more active toward THIS specific goal.

**The specificity vs generality test:** "To impress" reiterates the superobjective ("to win respect"). The sail doesn't need to look like the boat. Each beat should be a SPECIFIC step, not a restatement of the whole goal. "To arrive early" is specific. "To gain acknowledgment" is too general — it could apply at any point.

**Naming algorithm:**
1. State the beat as "to [verb] [object]"
2. Test: Is the verb more specific than the superobjective's verb? If not → too general → find a more specific verb.
3. Test: Could this beat name apply at multiple points in the story? If yes → too general → specify to THIS moment.
4. Test: Does this beat name generate specific images when you try the bar-story test? If not → too abstract → ground it in concrete action.

## 5. Breaking a Scene into Beats — The Algorithm

A beat is a single step toward the superobjective. Each beat is an essential building block — remove it and the story becomes incomprehensible.

**Step-by-step procedure:**

1. State the superobjective.
2. Ask: "What is the FIRST thing that must happen?" → Name it as "to [verb]."
3. Apply the **Removal Test**: Tell the story without this step. Breaks? → Keep. Works? → Cut.
4. Ask: "What is the NEXT ESSENTIAL step?" — test against the superobjective, NOT against "what happens next in the plot."
5. After each new beat, run:
   - Removal Test (is it essential?)
   - Circularity Check (does it repeat a previous beat?)
   - Superobjective Service Test (does it serve the throughline, not characterization?)
6. After all beats are listed, run:
   - Escalation Check: do stakes increase beat over beat?
   - Abstraction Level Check: are all beats at the same zoom level?
   - Completeness Check: does the final beat directly resolve or deny the superobjective?
7. Count: 3-8 beats. If <3, beats are too coarse. If >8, you have sub-beats or multiple scenes.

**The clue chain:** Each beat provides a CLUE to the next beat. The preparation beat clues you to the presentation beat (the notebook prepared in beat 2 is the notebook presented in beat 4). If a beat doesn't connect forward, it may be out of order or unnecessary.

**The turkey method:** Don't eat the whole turkey at once. Take off the drumstick (the scene). Take a bite (the beat). Finish that bite before taking another.

See `validation-engine.md` tests B1-B8 for the complete validation checklist.

## 6. Beat Naming Reference Examples

Beats should be named as verb phrases that describe a step toward the superobjective — not the result, not a description, not an emotion.

**Good beat names:**
- "to arrive early"
- "to prepare"
- "to pay homage"
- "to present the case"
- "to capitalize on a golden opportunity"
- "to rid the house of danger"

**Bad beat names (and why):**
- "Failed attempt" — that's a *result*, not an active step
- "To impress" — too close to the superobjective itself (the sail doesn't need to look like the boat)
- "To show self-confidence" — too abstract, could come at any point
- "To wait" — passive; "to prepare" is better because it's more active toward the superobjective
- "Judgment" — a state, not a step the protagonist takes

**The dynamic between beat and superobjective:** Each beat serves the superobjective, and each beat is complete in itself. If the beat only serves the superobjective, you have plodding narrative. If the beat only stands for itself, you have self-indulgent "performance art." The work of dramatic analysis is finding the dynamic where both moment and totality have strength.

## 7. The Iterative Process

The process of finding the throughline and beats is explicitly iterative:

1. Propose a throughline
2. Break it into beats
3. Discover the throughline might be wrong based on what the beats reveal
4. Reform the throughline
5. Revisit the beats in light of the new throughline
6. Repeat until the design fulfills all requirements

This is not a failure state — it IS the process. The work of analysis is "re-forming the large to better understand the small, and re-forming the small to better understand the large."

No angel will come down and tell you the throughline. You discover it through work.

## 8. When the Story Starts and Ends

**The story starts NOW because of a concrete external event:**
- The plague on Thebes (not because Oedipus "got curious")
- The big ears (Dumbo's problem he was born with)
- The pig attacks the child (not because the farmer "felt like selling")
- The death of Charles Foster Kane

Never start with backstory. Never start with "he suddenly gets an idea." The disordering event is external and concrete.

**The story ends when order is restored:**
- The pig is dealt with (not necessarily sold — the farmer rids himself of danger)
- The retraction is granted or categorically denied
- The plague is cured (though the cure has its own consequences)

The end of the syllogism: "If A (disorder), then B (restoration of order)." When B is achieved, go home.

**Getting in late, getting out early:** The best films start later than you'd expect and end sooner. "Burn the first reel" — twenty minutes in, the audience always thinks "they should have started here." Apply this to scenes too: enter after the scene has begun, exit before it's fully resolved (let the resolution happen in the next scene, pulling the audience forward).

## 9. Worked Example: "Getting a Retraction"

**Initial superobjective:** "Win the respect of the instructor"
**Problem:** Too vague — no definitive endpoint. When has respect been "won"?
**Reformed superobjective:** "Get a retraction" — concrete, testable, has a cap.

**Beat breakdown:**
1. **To arrive early** — establishes the protagonist's commitment (shots: man walks down hall, hand tries locked doorknob, man sits down)
2. **To prepare** — the protagonist readies his case (shots: man opens notebook, writes on tab, inserts tab, closes notebook)
3. **To pay homage** — deference to the authority figure (shots: feet walking in distant corridor, man stands up from seat)
4. **To present the case** — the concrete ask (shots: empty desk, notebook placed on desk, teacher looks down at notebook)
5. **Judgment** — the pivot point (shots: kids in doorway look in, teacher opens book, opens desk drawer, takes out stamp pad, stamps the book)
6. **Resolution** — order restored (shots: student smiling picks up book, hand closes book, wide shot of student going to seat as class enters)

**Key lessons from this example:**
- The throughline was reformed mid-process (from "gain respect" to "get a retraction") and this made every subsequent beat easier to find
- The notebook as prop is determined by the throughline: it must be *recognizable* (same notebook from beat 2), not "prepared-looking" or "contrite-looking"
- The student is not shown in the "present the case" beat — the notebook stands for the student. You don't have to follow the protagonist around.
- What the student writes on the tab is deliberately NOT shown — this withholds information, arouses curiosity, and respects the audience

## 10. Worked Example: "Selling the Pig"

**Initial superobjective:** "A man had to sell his pig"
**Deepened:** "A man had to rid his house of danger" (the pig attacked the child — this is more specific and emotionally resonant than just "sell a pig")

**Beat breakdown:**
1. **The disordering event** — child plays in yard, mom hears something, grabs broom, runs out (we NEVER see the pig attack — juxtaposition creates the idea of danger)
2. **The easy solution** — farmer leads pig to hillside, abandons it, walks home. Pig's POV: farmer walking away. Next shot: farmer approaches house. His POV: pig back in its stall. (The easy solution always fails.)
3. **Capitalizing on a golden opportunity** — farmer walking down road sees prosperous farmer fixing cart. Wipes pig's face with handkerchief, ties handkerchief on pig (capitalizing). Helps fix the cart (ingratiation). They talk, shake hands, new farmer puts pig in cart. Close-up of pig through bars. Pig jumps out. Farmer walking down road with pig again.
4. **End of the weary quest** — it's night now (because of all the failed attempts). Farmer arrives at slaughterhouse. Man closes office, locks up, walks away. Farmer tries door. Sits on steps with pig.
5. **Resolution: reward for a good deed** — morning. Farmer wakes, pig is gone. Conflict with slaughterhouse owner. Pig (still wearing handkerchief — recognizable!) is in the pen. Farmer takes pig back. Pig looks down the road. Farmer walks pig back toward slaughterhouse. Pays the owner. Owner enters pen, leads out another pig. Farmer walks home with TWO pigs. Wife sees them. Pigs enter barnyard. Two pigs kissing. Fade. Piglets suckling. "Our" pig (with handkerchief) giving the little girl a ride.

**The problem was "rid himself of danger." The solution was not selling the pig — it was removing the loneliness that made the pig dangerous.** Surprising and inevitable.
