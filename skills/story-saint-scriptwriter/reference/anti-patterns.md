## 1. Following the Protagonist Around

The dominant American approach: treat the camera as a record of what the protagonist does. This forces every moment to be "interesting" because the shot itself carries no dramatic weight.

**Symptoms:**
- Long takes following a character through spaces
- The camera "discovers" things as the character discovers them
- Every scene is essentially surveillance footage of an actor performing
- You find yourself asking "what's the most interesting way to shoot this?"

**The fix:** Ask "what is this scene ABOUT?" instead of "how do I follow this character?" The scene is not about a person in a room. It's about a specific step toward a specific objective. Film THAT.

## 2. "Interesting" Angles and Shots

"From above." "A bird's-eye view." "A Dutch angle to show disorientation." These are all attempts to be interesting, which is to say, to compensate for a lack of dramatic structure.

**The rule:** Always do things the least interesting way. The most blunt way. The least interesting angle. Because:
- "Interesting" always comes from the structure, not the photography
- The audience is collectively smarter than the director and has already gotten ahead to the punch line
- Novel angles draw attention to the filmmaking and away from the story
- Your desire to be interesting is actually your conscious mind's desire to be liked

**"From above" is a novel angle.** Novel is not the same as correct. The correct angle is the one that captures the uninflected image needed by the shot list. Period.

## 3. Narration Disguised as Drama

Narration says: "you'll never guess why what I just told you is important to the story." It shows up in many forms:

**Visual narration:**
- Jodie looks at watch. Dissolve. Thirty hours later. (Can't be filmed without explanation)
- A sign saying "Elmville General Hospital" (the audience won't read it)
- A pan down a building to establish location
- A character looking meaningfully at an object

**Verbal narration:**
- "Hello Jack, I'm coming over because I need the money you borrowed"
- ADR (dubbed dialogue) to explain what the film failed to show
- Characters describing their own state: "as hip as I am, it's been a trial sitting here for thirty hours"

**Prop/costume narration:**
- Trying to make a notebook look like a "retraction notebook"
- Dressing a character to "show" their personality
- Set design that "tells" the audience about the character's life

**The fix:** Every time you catch yourself explaining or establishing, ask: "Is this essential to the story, or am I trying to help the audience?" If you're helping, stop. They don't need it.

## 4. Establishing Shots and Signs

"But we won't know where we are!" — the universal Hollywood whine. Mamet's response: in thousands of movies you've seen, when have you EVER not known where you were? Never.

**Signs don't work** because:
- The audience doesn't want to read signs; they want to watch a story
- The audience will go to the most interesting thing in the frame, which is always what happens next in the story
- Signs are after-the-fact attempts to shore up what wasn't correctly designed

**ADR doesn't work** for the same reason — you can't force the audience to listen to explanatory dubbed dialogue any more than you can force them to read a sign.

**The fix:** If the audience needs to know it's a school, show the things that ARE a school — desks, a hallway with lockers, a bell ringing. But only if those things serve the throughline. Usually, the audience will figure out where they are from context, because that's what human perception does automatically.

## 5. Characterization Over Action

"Character" does not exist apart from habitual action. There is no "inner life" to reveal, no "backstory" to establish, no "character arc" to develop. Character is EXACTLY what the person does in pursuit of the superobjective.

**Symptoms of characterization disease:**
- "Nick, a young fellow in his thirties with a flair for the unusual" (unfimable)
- "A fellow, trim, fit, obviously enamored of the good things of life but not without a certain somberness" (unfimable)
- "He's the wacky kind of guy who..." (unfimable and alienating — we identify with pursuit of goals, not with quirks)
- Scenes designed to "show who the character really is"
- An actor asking "what's my motivation?" or "what am I thinking?"

**The fix:** Character is revealed through action toward the objective. Period. The audience identifies with the protagonist because they identify with the *pursuit of a goal* — not because the character has been described or characterized. The less the hero is inflected, the more the audience identifies.

## 6. Circularity and Repetition

Never climb a stair you've already climbed. Never play the same beat twice in different guises.

**Circularity** is the signature of:
- The epic form (not the dramatic form)
- Autobiography (events of similar type recurring over time)
- Lazy writing ("things keep going wrong" as a substitute for dramatic progression)

**The Leadbelly test:** In the blues, the knife cuts bread in verse one, shaves in verse two, and kills in verse three. Same knife, escalating stakes. If the knife cuts bread in verse one and cuts cheese in verse two, we already know it can cut bread — what else can it do?

**The fix:** Each beat must represent a NEW step at a higher level of stakes. "Arriving early" and "waiting" are too similar. "Preparing" and "reviewing preparation" are too similar. Move forward or you lose the audience.

## 7. The Steadicam Fallacy

The Steadicam "has done injury to American movies" because it makes it easy to follow the protagonist around without thinking about what the shot IS.

**The fallacy:** "I can shoot the whole thing in one take this morning." It feels efficient. It looks interesting in dailies. But in the editing room, you discover you have no cuts, no juxtaposition, no shots that can be reordered to tell the story.

**Extended form of the fallacy:** Wanting locations to be literally contiguous — the hall literally around the corner from the door, the door literally opening onto the staircase — so the camera can follow from one to the next. This undermines the essential nature of film: disparate shots, cut together.

**The fix:** If you love the morning's Steadicam work at dailies, you'll hate it in the editing room. Shoot uninflected, short shots that can cut together. The story lives in the cuts.

## 8. Two Reasons = No Reason

If the farmer needs to sell the pig because (a) the pig attacked his daughter AND (b) the family is broke, you have two reasons — which is equal to no reason.

"I was late because the bus drivers are on strike AND my aunt fell downstairs." Split focus. Neither reason is fully convincing because neither needs to be — the other one is carrying the slack.

**The fix:** One reason. One throughline. One superobjective. If you have two reasons, pick the stronger one and cut the other. The story will be more powerful with less.

## 9. Backstory

Backstory is what the illiterate call the history of the character. You don't need it.

**The dirty joke doesn't begin:** "Who would think that the two most disparate occupations of agriculture and salesmanship would one day be united in our oral literature?"

**The dirty joke begins:** "A traveling salesman stops at a farmer's door."

The protagonist doesn't need to explain WHY he wants a retraction. To whom would he explain it? To the audience? Does that help him get it? No. He must only do those things that help him get a retraction.

"The guy says to the girl, 'that's a lovely dress'" — he does NOT say, "I haven't been laid in six weeks."

**The fix:** Start with the disordering event. The audience doesn't need to know why — they need to know what happens next. Backstory is the writer's tool for understanding the story, not the audience's tool for watching it.

## 10. Asking the Wrong Questions

**Wrong questions:**
- "What's the most interesting way to film this?" (Interest comes from structure, not novelty)
- "How do I establish the character?" (You don't — character is action)
- "Where did the character just come from?" (Can't be acted, doesn't matter)
- "What is the character thinking?" (Can't be filmed)
- "How do I make this more interesting?" (You're already lost)

**Right questions:**
- "What is this scene ABOUT?" (The superobjective)
- "What is this beat about?" (The step toward the superobjective)
- "What two uninflected images juxtaposed will create this idea?" (The shot list)
- "Is this essential? What happens if I remove it?" (The Occam test)
- "Would I like to know what happens next?" (The audience test)
