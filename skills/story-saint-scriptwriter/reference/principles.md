## 1. Eisenstein's Montage Theory

The foundation of Mamet's directing method is Eisenstein's theory of montage: **a movie is a succession of images juxtaposed so that the contrast between these images moves the story forward in the mind of the audience.**

This is opposed to the dominant American approach of "following the protagonist around" — treating the camera as a record of what real people really did. Mamet considers that approach fundamentally flawed because it forces the director to make everything "interesting" rather than letting the story carry itself.

**The cut tells the story.** Not the shot. Not the acting. Not the dialogue. The juxtaposition of image A and image B creates idea C in the viewer's mind. Example: shot of birds snapping a twig + shot of a fawn raising its head = the idea of "alertness to danger." The shots were filmed miles and years apart. They are unrelated. But their juxtaposition creates meaning.

**Algorithmic formulation:** SHOT_A + CUT + SHOT_B → IDEA_C, where IDEA_C is not contained in SHOT_A or SHOT_B individually. If IDEA_C can be derived from either shot alone, the other shot is redundant. If IDEA_C is the beat concept, the juxtaposition is correct.

## 2. The Conscious/Unconscious Rule

"The purpose of technique is to free the unconscious."

This is Mamet's core mechanism for WHY the algorithmic approach works. The conscious mind wants to be liked, wants to be interesting, and will default to clichés because they offer the security of having succeeded before. The conscious mind generates: "interesting angles," "meaningful looks," "establishing shots," "character moments."

The unconscious mind, when freed by rigorous structure, generates genuine creativity — surprising images, unexpected juxtapositions, connections that feel inevitable but weren't predictable.

**The mechanism:** If you follow the rules rigorously (throughline → beats → shots, each validated), your conscious mind is occupied with the technical task. This frees the unconscious to do the creative work. "Good luck will be the residue of good design" — meaning the seemingly arbitrary decisions you make AFTER doing the structural work will actually be subconscious solutions to problems you've been wrestling with.

**Decision rule:** When you find yourself reaching for something "interesting" or "meaningful" — a clever angle, an expressive shot, a loaded moment — that's your conscious mind trying to be liked. STOP. Return to the algorithm. Ask: "What is this beat about? What two uninflected images create that idea?" Let the algorithm generate the answer.

## 3. Uninflected Images

The best image is an uninflected image. A shot of a teacup. A shot of a spoon. A shot of a door. These are building blocks — they carry no embedded meaning on their own. Meaning emerges only from juxtaposition.

**Why uninflected?** Because the more you "load" a shot with meaning (a "respectful angle," an actor performing "contrite"), the less powerful the cut becomes. You're doing the audience's work for them, and they resent it. Worse, you're narrating rather than storytelling.

Two guys walking down the street. One says something to the other. That's a shot list. It's uninflected. You're listening because you want to know what happens next. The moment you inflect — "two guys walking purposefully down a rain-slicked street, their faces etched with determination" — you've switched from storytelling to decoration.

**The nail doesn't have to look like a house.** Each component does its own job. The shot conveys its simple image. The beat conveys its step. The scene conveys its objective. The film conveys its story. Make each part do its job, and the whole emerges "as if by magic."

## 4. The Dream Analogy

Film operates like a dream. Dreams are composed of vastly varied, magnificently interesting images — most of them uninflected. It's their juxtaposition that gives the dream its power. The terror and beauty come from connecting previously unrelated mundanities of life.

A film should be equally free. Discontinuous images, juxtaposed, create a third idea that neither image contains alone. This is why Mamet recommends reading Freud's *The Interpretation of Dreams*, Bettelheim's *The Uses of Enchantment*, and Jung's *Memories, Dreams, Reflections*.

The audience's subconscious processes the film the same way it processes a dream — ordering events into meaning automatically. The filmmaker's job is to work with this natural process, not against it.

## 5. Story vs. Narration

**Narration is the enemy.** Narration says: "you'll never guess why what I just told you is important." Story says: "here's what happened next."

Signs of narration creeping in:
- A character describes themselves or their situation aloud
- A shot is designed to "establish" location or character
- Camera angles are chosen to convey mood rather than information
- Dialogue carries plot information that the images should carry
- Props or costumes are designed to "express" the character's state

**The perfect movie has no dialogue.** Dialogue is "sprinkles on the ice cream cone" — a gloss on what's already being told through the shots. If you're telling the story with pictures, bad dialogue makes the movie somewhat worse and good dialogue makes it somewhat better, but the story survives intact — which is why great films survive dubbing and subtitling.

Almost all movie scripts contain material that cannot be filmed: "Nick, a young fellow in his thirties with a flair for the unusual." You can't photograph a flair for the unusual. You can only photograph what a person does.

## 6. The Audience's Perception Engine

The audience will accept anything they have not been given a reason to disbelieve. They don't need "establishing shots" — when have you ever sat in a movie and said "I don't know where I am"? Never. You always know. You always figure it out, because the human mind demands to create order from images.

**The neurosis model:** Mamet draws a direct analogy between how film works and how neurosis works. In neurosis, the mind takes unrelated events and orders them under an overriding preconception: "I am unattractive → she rushed past me in the hall → therefore she rejected me." The brain CANNOT HELP creating causal chains from sequential events. This is the engine that makes film work.

**Algorithmic implication:** If the overriding preconception is "a story is being told" (which it is, the moment the screen lights up), the audience will AUTOMATICALLY order every image into that story. They will create cause-and-effect from juxtaposition whether the filmmaker designs it or not. This means:
- You don't need to EXPLAIN connections — the audience makes them automatically
- You don't need to ESTABLISH contexts — the audience infers them
- You DO need to SEQUENCE correctly — because the audience will create meaning from whatever sequence you provide, including wrong meaning from bad sequence
- Bad films "work" at a basic level because this perception engine runs automatically — but the audience isn't invested because their conscious and unconscious are out of alignment

**When the film is correctly designed:** The conscious and unconscious are in alignment. The audience orders events the same way the author did. They become invested. They need to know what happens next.

**When the film is badly designed:** The audience still orders events (they can't help it), but the ordering feels arbitrary or forced. They create their own story (like a neurotic creating false cause-and-effect), but they're not willing to invest emotionally. "I'm following it, but I'm not going to risk my unconscious well-being by becoming involved."

**The MacGuffin principle** (from Hitchcock): the less the objective is described, the more the audience fills in their own meaning. "The secret documents" is enough. The less the hero is characterized, the more the audience identifies with them. "A hero rode up on a white horse" — not a short hero, not a tall hero. Just a hero. This works BECAUSE of the perception engine — the audience's subconscious supplies the details that make it personal.

## 7. Countercultural Architecture Warning

Mamet draws a sustained analogy between bad filmmaking and 1960s countercultural architecture — buildings designed around how the architect "felt" rather than around the building's purpose. These buildings proved unlivable. They all either fell down or should be torn down.

The same happens with "personal expression" filmmaking: panning around to show how moved the director is, "deeply felt" shots that serve the director's ego rather than the story. These are like flat roofs in Vermont — they ignore the nature of the materials (human perception) and the purpose of the structure (telling a story).

Traditional dramatic structure exists for the same reason traditional architecture does: it works. Doors are placed in certain ways for a reason. Beats follow beats for a reason. The job is not to express yourself but to serve the inhabitants (the audience).

## 8. The Dirty Joke Model

Drama is a specialized form of fairy tale, and the fairy tale is a specialized form of the dirty joke. "A traveling salesman stops at a farmer's door" — that's how drama begins. Not with backstory, not with characterization, not with elaborate scene-setting.

The dirty joke model teaches:
- Start with the disordering event ("once upon a time...")
- Each step escalates (Leadbelly's knife: first verse cuts bread, second verse shaves, third verse kills)
- The ending is both surprising and inevitable
- No step is wasted on decoration
- You know when it's over

Fairy tales are told in the simplest images, without elaboration, without characterization. The characterization is left to the audience. This is the model for film.
