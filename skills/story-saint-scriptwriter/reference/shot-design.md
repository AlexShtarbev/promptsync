## 1. The Uninflected Shot

The shot is the smallest unit of film. It must be:

- **Simple** — one image of one thing. A man walking. A hand on a doorknob. An empty desk. A notebook being placed.
- **Uninflected** — no embedded emotional or narrative meaning. Not "a man walking purposefully" or "a hand trembling on a doorknob." Just a man walking. Just a hand on a doorknob.
- **Functional** — it exists to be juxtaposed with other shots to create an idea. It does not exist to be "interesting" on its own.

The more you inflect or load a shot, the less powerful the cut becomes. A loaded shot does the audience's work for them and weakens the montage.

**Test for inflection:** Can you describe the shot in under 8 words without any adjectives that convey emotion or narrative? "Man walks down hall." "Hand tries doorknob." "Empty room." If you need mood words, the shot is inflected.

## 2. Juxtaposition Creates Meaning

The entire theory in one sentence: **two uninflected images, placed next to each other, create a third idea in the audience's mind that neither image contains alone.**

Examples:
- Birds snapping a twig + fawn raising its head = alertness to danger
- Feet walking in distant corridor + man stands up = homage/attentiveness
- Child playing in yard + mom grabbing broom and running = danger (the attack itself is never shown)
- Empty desk + notebook placed on it + teacher looking down = presenting a case

This is the mechanism of both dreams and documentary filmmaking. Documentaries take unrelated footage shot days apart and juxtapose it to create meaning. The fiction filmmaker has the advantage of being able to shoot exactly the uninflected images needed.

## 3. The Bar-Story Test

When you're stuck finding shots for a beat, tell the story as if you're describing it to someone at a bar.

"So this guy comes into a room, and he's early, right? The door's locked. He sits down and waits." That's already a shot list:
1. Man walks down hall
2. Hand tries doorknob (locked)
3. Man sits down

People naturally tell stories cinematically — they jump-cut between images. Trust that instinct. The bar-story strips away all the literary narration and gives you the essential images.

**Warning:** The bar-story test gives you a first draft. You then refine by applying the uninflection principle — strip out any "interesting" details that crept in, and test each image against the beat's objective.

## 4. What You Can and Cannot Photograph

You can photograph:
- A man walking
- A hand on a door
- A notebook on a desk
- A pig in a pen
- A woman grabbing a broom

You CANNOT photograph:
- "A young fellow in his thirties with a flair for the unusual"
- "A brash hipster who's been sitting on the bench for thirty hours"
- A character's backstory
- A character's inner state
- "Where he just came from"
- "What he's thinking"

If you find that a point cannot be made without narration (visual or verbal), it is virtually certain that the point is unimportant to the story. The audience requires not information but drama.

**Character does not exist** apart from habitual action (Aristotle). "Character" is exactly what the person literally does in pursuit of the superobjective. Everything else is decoration. You never have to "establish the character."

## 5. Withholding Information

The audience's attention is held by withholding information, not by providing it. Every time you explain something, you tax the audience's goodwill.

**The notebook example:** The student writes something on a tab. We do NOT read what he writes. This arouses curiosity — "what is he writing? What is he preparing for?" The audience is now in the same position as the protagonist: anxious for something to happen.

**The pig attack example:** We never see the pig attack the child. We see: child playing, mom's head snaps around, mom grabs broom, runs out. Then: farmer leading pig down the road. The attack happened in the *cut* — in the audience's mind. This is more powerful than any on-screen violence.

General principle: **show less, mean more.** If you suggest the idea through juxtaposition, the audience creates a version more powerful than anything you could show, because it's *their* version, drawn from *their* subconscious.

## 6. Props, Costumes, and Sets Through the Throughline

When the prop person asks "what does it look like?" — the answer comes from the throughline, not from the character, the mood, or the setting.

**Right question:** "What does this object need to DO in the shot list?" The notebook needs to be recognizable as the same notebook from the preparation beat. That's the most important thing about it.

**Wrong question:** "What kind of notebook would a character seeking a retraction carry?" You can't make a "retraction notebook." You can't make a "contrite notebook." You can make a *recognizable* notebook.

Props must convey **that thing without which the audience cannot understand the story.** Everything else is antiseptic (neatness so the audience doesn't question sincerity) rather than creative.

The same applies to sets: you don't need to "establish" a farm. The audience knows what a farm looks like. What you need is the specific visual elements that serve the throughline — the empty pens in the barn, the single pig in one pen, the nearly-empty feed sack.

## 7. Where to Put the Camera

The answer is: **"over there" — in the place where it will capture the uninflected shot needed to tell the story.**

There is no such thing as a "respectful angle" or an "ominous angle." The camera goes where it needs to go to photograph the simple image required by the shot list.

If you don't know which position is better than another, let your subconscious choose. You've earned that right by doing the structural work. "Good luck will be the residue of good design."

**Fight the desire for literal contiguity.** The hall doesn't need to literally be around the corner from the door. The door doesn't need to literally open onto the staircase. Film is made of disparate shots, cut together. Each shot is photographed simply, wherever convenient. Wanting objects literally next to each other is the Steadicam fallacy — it feels efficient but undermines the essential nature of film.

## 8. What to Tell the Actors

Tell the actor the simple physical action required by the beat. Nothing more.

"Walk down the hall. Try the doorknob. Sit down."

Not: "Walk down the hall *respectfully*." Not: "Try the doorknob *anxiously*." Not: "Sit down *with determination*."

**The actor doesn't need to know:**
- "What am I thinking here?"
- "What's my motivation?"
- "Where did I just come from?"

None of these can be acted. You can't act "where you just came from." The script is doing the emotional work through structure and juxtaposition. The actor's job is to perform simple physical actions simply.

**The Casablanca example:** Humphrey Bogart was told "stand on the balcony, when I say 'action,' take a beat and nod." He nodded. That's great acting. The audience is moved by simple restraint in an emotional situation.

The choice of actions and adverbs constitutes the entire craft of directing actors:
- Walk quickly / slowly / determinedly
- Sit / stand / turn / look / pick up / put down

That's the vocabulary. Everything else is the actor's ego and the scene's structure.

## 9. Dialogue as Sprinkles

Dialogue exists only because people speak to get what they want. Not to convey information, not to reveal character, not to establish setting.

**Bad film dialogue:** "Hello, Jack, I'm coming over to your home this evening because I need to get the money you borrowed from me."

**Good film dialogue:** "Where the hell were you yesterday?"

The difference: the good version gives the audience no information except the emotional charge. The story — told through shots — provides the context. Dialogue is a gloss on visual storytelling, not a replacement for it.

**Strive to make a silent movie.** If the story works with no dialogue, the structure is sound. Dialogue can then make it somewhat better or somewhat worse, but the foundation holds.

## 10. Show vs. Suggest

Every time you show something "real" on screen, the audience has one of two reactions:
1. "That's fake" — breaks immersion
2. "That's real!" — breaks immersion in a different way

Both take the audience out of the story. The solution: **suggest through juxtaposition.**

Don't show the car crash — show the pedestrian walking, the onlooker's head turning, the driver looking up, the foot on the brake, the legs at a strange angle under the car (Pudovkin). The audience assembles "accident" from these uninflected images.

Don't show the pig attacking — show the child playing, the mother's alarm, then the farmer leading the pig away. The attack happened between cuts.

This principle applies to all dramatic moments: romance, violence, revelation, confrontation. The less you show, the more the audience creates, and the more powerful the result.
