# Methodology

Four sources you lean on constantly. Use them as thinking tools, not rigid rules.

---

## Robert McKee — *Story*

### Controlling Idea

One sentence in the form: **"Life becomes X when the hero does Y, because Z."** Every scene must serve it.

If you can't state the controlling idea in one sentence, the film has no spine. Idea first, scenes second.

### Scene Value (Movement of Value)

In every scene **something changes** — from + to – or vice versa. A value enters the scene in one state and leaves in another.

**A scene without a value turn is not a scene.** It's just description.

Before writing a scene, answer:
- What value enters? (e.g. trust)
- In what state? (the team trusts the leader)
- In what state does it leave? (the team suspects the leader)
- How? (what action flips the value)

Common value pairs:
- Control ↔ helplessness
- Trust ↔ betrayal
- Love ↔ obsession
- Life ↔ death
- Freedom ↔ captivity
- Discipline ↔ chaos
- Comrade ↔ instrument
- Hope ↔ despair
- Knowledge ↔ ignorance

### Inciting Incident

The event that knocks the hero's life out of balance. Must happen **in the first quarter** of the film (for a 90-min feature: by minute 22).

Without it, the plot does not start. The hero's life BEFORE and AFTER is two different worlds.

### Progressive Complications

Each next conflict is harder than the last. The gap between the hero's expectation and the result of his action keeps widening.

If your third-act conflict is weaker than your first-act conflict, the structure is broken.

### Crisis → Climax → Resolution

- **Crisis:** the hero's last dilemma. A choice between two incompatible values. The hardest point.
- **Climax:** the action that answers the dilemma. The most physical and loudest beat in the film.
- **Resolution:** a short coordinate of the new world after the climax.

### The +/− Pendulum

The hero can't be winning the whole time, or losing the whole time. Alternate. Amplitude grows with each iteration.

In a fall arc the +'s become local (small wins), the –'s systemic (large losses). In a rise arc, the inverse.

### Show, Don't Tell

Replace internal monologue with action and objects. An object in frame says more than a line.

A pregnancy test instead of "I'm pregnant." A torn letter instead of "he left her." An empty chair instead of "he is alone."

## Joseph Campbell — *The Hero with a Thousand Faces*

Use Campbell **as diagnostic, not dogma**. Not every story is a clean hero's journey. If you're writing an anti-hero or a tragedy, the mirror journey works — *katabasis* with no return, like Orpheus losing Eurydice.

### The 12 Stages

1. **Ordinary World** — hero in equilibrium. Show what is, so the audience knows what will be lost.
2. **Call to Adventure** — event demanding the hero leave home.
3. **Refusal of the Call** — hero hesitates, fears. Without this beat the hero is a function, not a character.
4. **Meeting the Mentor** — someone gives a tool, skill or knowledge. Can be a false mentor = shadow.
5. **Crossing the Threshold** — hero crosses the boundary between worlds.
6. **Tests, Allies, Enemies** — settling into the new world.
7. **Approach to the Cave** — preparation for the main test.
8. **The Ordeal** — meeting with death or shadow.
9. **The Reward** — hero gains what he came for.
10. **The Road Back** — return to the ordinary world (or in anti-hero: impossibility of return).
11. **Resurrection** — final test. Hero dies and is reborn.
12. **Return with the Elixir** — hero brings the world something new.

### Archetypes

- **Hero** — active engine of the plot.
- **Shadow** — anti-hero, reflection of the hero's dark side.
- **Mentor** — gives the tool.
- **Threshold Guardian** — obstacle at the entrance to the new world.
- **Herald** — announces the call.
- **Shapeshifter** — switches sides.
- **Trickster** — comic figure who breaks the rules.

In an anti-hero tragedy, **the mentor often = the shadow**. The hero doesn't realise his teacher is his enemy.

## Aristotle — *Poetics*

### Hamartia (ἁμαρτία) — the fatal flaw

Not "sin" in a moral sense, but a flaw in character that triggers the tragedy. A good hero has a weak trait that in normal life is a virtue, but in extraordinary circumstances becomes catastrophe.

- "I love too hard" — hamartia of romantic tragedy.
- "I trust everyone" — hamartia of political tragedy.
- "I cannot forgive" — hamartia of family tragedy.

The hamartia must be **visible from the first scene** and **explode at the climax**.

### Peripeteia (περιπέτεια) — reversal of action

An action meant to bring X brings −X.

Wishing to save, kills. Wishing to protect, destroys. Wishing to open, closes forever.

A strong film has 2-3 peripeteias, escalating in force.

### Anagnorisis (ἀναγνώρισις) — recognition

The moment when the hero (or audience) understands what was hidden.

The best anagnorisis **coincides with the peripeteia.** Double blow: the hero recognises the truth at the same moment his action reverses on him.

### Catharsis (κάθαρσις) — purification through pity and fear

The ending must produce in the audience a mixed feeling: sympathy for the hero and fear for the self ("that could be me").

### Unity of Action

Aristotle demands that everything in the tragedy lead to one point. Every action, every object, every line must serve the final point.

**If a scene does not serve the final point, the scene is extraneous.** The test is simple: remove the scene; does the film change? If not, cut.

## Brandon Sanderson — Promise / Progress / Payoff

Use this especially for short-form. It maps cleanly onto 15/30/60/90s pieces but also works as a top-line check on long-form acts.

### Promise / Progress / Payoff

- **PROMISE:** The opening hook sets tone, conflict, and stakes. First 1-3 seconds of a Short, first 5-10 minutes of a feature. Four types: tone, story, character, structural.
- **PROGRESS:** The middle shows movement. Every beat must raise stakes, reveal information, or shift emotion. If you can remove the middle and the ending still makes sense, progress isn't working.
- **PAYOFF:** The ending delivers on the promise. Must feel **earned**. A twist that contradicts the promise is a broken contract.

**Big P vs little p.** The *Big P* plot is the overarching question ("what is this about?"); the *little p* plots are the scene/beat-level problems that accumulate into it. In short-form the Big P is the whole piece's promise; the little p's are the individual beats.

**Progress types — track the right kind.** "Progress" is concrete differently per plot — name which axis the story runs on, then make every beat visibly advance *that* one:
- **Information** (mystery/thriller): clues surface, suspects fall away, the unknown narrows.
- **Relationship** (romance/buddy): conflict → understanding → connection.
- **Character-internal**: a belief is questioned, a skill grows, a wound is approached.

**Signposting.** Plant breadcrumbs that tell the audience "you're making progress" — a narrowing list, a counter, a relationship thawing. Without signposts the middle feels like running in place even when it isn't.

**The twist mechanism (the "Gandalf Promise").** A twist is a promise *redirected*, not broken — set the expectation, then make the payoff seem **impossible** right before delivering it. The bigger the apparent impossibility, the bigger the catharsis. A twist that *contradicts* (rather than recontextualizes) the promise breaks the contract.

### Character: Proactive, Relatable, Capable

Three **sliders, not checkboxes.** The most compelling characters don't max all three — they shine in one, sit moderate in a second, and start *low* on the third. **The underdeveloped axis is the arc** (Luke: low capability → grows it). Dialing one too high costs another — excess capability drains relatability (the flawless hero reads as smug or dull).

- **PROACTIVE:** drives the story by making choices; passive characters kill engagement. **The Villain Problem:** antagonists are often more proactive than heroes — fix it by giving the protagonist early small proactive moments (solving an ordinary problem cleverly) and a specific, concrete want. For **constrained characters** (imprisoned, powerless), show *mental* proactivity — planning, small acts of agency, attempts that fail — so they still drive.
- **RELATABLE:** the audience connects through empathy or recognition. Fast techniques: **save-the-cat** (they help someone), **self-awareness** (they own a flaw out loud), **universal detail** (a feeling everyone shares — Garfield hating Mondays), **outside-view** (someone else sees the good in them). Note: **relatable ≠ likable** — an unpleasant character can still be deeply relatable.
- **CAPABLE:** competent at something specific, with clear weaknesses elsewhere — that contrast is the sweet spot. Total competence reads as comedy or wish-fulfillment.

**Hero vs protagonist:** a *hero* does virtuous things; a *protagonist* is simply who we root for. Antiheroes run high-capability / low-relatability — to make one work, make their opponents even *less* relatable than they are.

**Motivation is the master dial.** "The difference between a wooden character and a rounded one is motivation." When a character isn't working, ~60% of the time it's a motivation problem — not voice or plot. Check: have you shown what they want? Do their actions match it? Can the audience follow their decisions?

Always define **WANT** (external goal) and **NEED** (internal growth requirement). Then map the obstacle as one of three distinct **weakness types** — don't collapse them into one "flaw":
- **FLAW** — a defect they must *overcome* to grow (cowardice, pride).
- **RESTRICTION** — a self-imposed rule they choose *not* to break (a code, a vow); drama comes from pressure on it.
- **LIMITATION** — an external constraint they can't change, only navigate (no money, a disability, the law).

WANT/NEED/FLAW maps onto McKee's *desire / true desire* and Aristotle's *hamartia*.

Arc types:
- **TRANSFORMATIONAL** — character changes (moves along a P/R/C slider).
- **ICONIC** — character stays the same, the world changes around them.

### Character Identity & Customization

P/R/C is the *mechanical* layer (how a character functions in plot). Identity is the *presentation* layer — how the audience experiences them.

- **The identity triad: Motivation / Personality / Values.** Motivation = what they want; Personality = how they express it; Values = the principles that explain otherwise-inexplicable choices (Magneto's plan reads as protection, through his trauma). Values are what make a villain's logic land.
- **Iceberg model.** Know the whole character; show only the tip. The submerged 90% — backstory, secrets, the failure they won't repeat, how they self-blame — is *felt* through behavior, not stated.
- **Hollywood Four-Question** (fast depth): what do they *want*, why *can't* they have it, what do they *need*, what must they *sacrifice*? The sacrifice question is the one most often skipped — and the one that lands the climax.
- **Quirks.** Audiences remember a character by their quirk first, then add depth. A quirk must connect to the core (an impulsive character's quirk reinforces impulsiveness); a quirk bolted on for color feels hollow.
- **Voice test.** Strip the dialogue tags from a three-character scene — if the reader can't tell who's speaking, the voices aren't distinct (see `style-rules.md` → Character Voice for the parameters).

### Conflict and Escalation

- **Try-fail via Yes-But / No-And.** Each attempt resolves as **"yes, BUT"** (they succeed — but a complication makes things worse) or **"no, AND"** (they fail — and the stakes worsen). A flat "yes" ends the story; a flat "no" stalls it. Classic three-beat: obvious solution fails → complex attempt fails and reveals a limit → success at real cost. Use even in micro-stories.
- Escalation is mandatory — the *nature* or *severity* of the threat must shift, not just repeat.
- Layer conflicts: **EXTERNAL** (visible problem), **INTERNAL** (emotional struggle), **RELATIONAL** (tension between characters). Two layers minimum.
- Quiet stories still need emotional movement — a shift from one state to another.
- **Hang a lantern** on an unavoidable oddity or coincidence — have a character notice it out loud — so the audience reads it as authorial control, not a mistake.

### Structure — the Story Circle

"A structure is something you can hang your story on" — it gives consistency without dictating creativity. Be a **chef, not a cook**: learn the principles so you can improvise, rather than following a recipe rigidly. Sanderson favours Dan Harmon's **Story Circle** — a flexible 8-beat compaction of the Hero's Journey that scales from a novel to a 30-second Short:

1. **You** — a character in their comfort zone (establishes normal + the lack).
2. **Need** — they want something.
3. **Go** — they cross into an unfamiliar situation.
4. **Search** — they adapt, try, struggle (the try-fail middle).
5. **Find** — they get what they wanted.
6. **Take** — they pay its price.
7. **Return** — back to the familiar, changed.
8. **Change** — they can now do what they couldn't at step 1.

Top half (1–4) is the descent into the unknown; bottom half (5–8) is the changed return. For short-form, collapse to four beats: comfort → go → find-at-a-cost → changed. It is a compaction of the Campbell column in the cross-framework map, and folds onto Promise (1–2) / Progress (3–5) / Payoff (6–8).

### Sanderson's Laws

- **First Law:** Audience satisfaction with a resolution is proportional to their understanding of the rules beforehand. In a 30-second Short, establish the rule in the first 5 seconds.
- **Second Law:** Limitations are more interesting than powers.
- **Third Law:** Expand what exists before adding new elements. One well-explored idea beats five shallow ones.

### Re-Watch Triggers

- Plant details that only make sense after the ending.
- Write dual-meaning hooks (mean one thing at start, another after the reveal).
- Hide visual foreshadowing in the first frame.
- Diagnostic: "If the viewer watches twice, will the second viewing be a different experience?"

## HOW TO USE

Before writing any scene, run this checklist:

1. What is the controlling idea (McKee)?
2. What value enters this scene, and what value leaves?
3. Which Campbell stage are we in (if applicable)?
4. How does this scene work the hero's hamartia / weakness (flaw, restriction, or limitation)?
5. Is this a peripeteia scene or a setup scene?
6. Does each attempt resolve as **yes-but** or **no-and** — not a flat yes or no?
7. Is the driving character's **motivation** clear here? (When a beat feels wooden, suspect motivation first — ~60% of the time.)
8. (Short-form only) What is the Promise / Progress / Payoff for this beat, and which **progress type** (information / relationship / character-internal) does it advance?

If any answer is empty, the scene is either unnecessary or needs to be re-thought before you write it.

## CROSS-FRAMEWORK MAP

These four frameworks overlap. Use this map so you don't double up. The **Story Circle** (above) is a compaction of the Campbell column — reach for it instead of the full 12 stages when the runtime is short.

| McKee | Aristotle | Campbell | Sanderson |
|---|---|---|---|
| Inciting Incident | — | Call to Adventure | Promise (story hook) |
| Progressive Complications | Peripeteia (chained) | Tests, Approach | Progress (escalation) |
| Crisis | — | Ordeal | (climactic moment of choice) |
| Climax | Climax / Anagnorisis | Resurrection | Payoff |
| Resolution | Catharsis | Return with Elixir | Earned ending |
| Hero's Desire vs True Desire | Hamartia | Hero / Shadow | WANT / NEED / FLAW |
