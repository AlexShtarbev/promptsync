# Style Rules

How to write the page itself. These rules are non-negotiable. They apply to every format: feature, pilot, Short, sketch, sketch within a series.

---

## GENERAL RULES

1. **Action verbs. No description.**
   The camera films only what is seen and heard. No "he feels," no "she understands," no "memories flood his mind," no "the atmosphere grows tense." Only verbs (HITS, EXITS, GRABS, STARES, WHISPERS).

2. **Brevity = talent.** If you can say it in two words, say it in two. Every extra line is an extra minute of film.

3. **Change only what was asked.** Targeted edits. No "improving" surrounding lines.

4. **Hollywood format.**
   - **Slug-line:** ALL CAPS, bold, left margin
   - **Action:** left margin, present tense, action verbs only
   - **Character cue:** ALL CAPS, indent ≈ 2.2" from margin (3168 DXA)
   - **Parenthetical:** indent ≈ 1.6" from margin (2304 DXA)
   - **Dialogue:** indent 1" left + 1.5" right (1440 / 2160 DXA)
   - **Transition:** right margin, ALL CAPS

5. **One version, not five.** When you propose a scene, give one version + one argument. If the user rejects it, ask one narrow binary question, then give the next single version.

6. **Slug-line format:** `INT./EXT. — LOCATION — TIME OF DAY`. Time of day is usually DAWN / DAY / DUSK / NIGHT. Slug-lines are **always** in one language (chosen at project start) — never bilingual.

---

## BAD vs GOOD

Bad: "A grey dawn paints the mountain peaks pink. An ancient temple looms over the chasm."

Good: "EXT. MOUNTAINS — DAWN. Three dots streak across a snow ridge."

---

Bad: "The hero feels his heart breaking from the pain of loss. His eyes fill with longing."

Good: "HERO STARES at the mountain." (Whatever he feels, the actor plays.)

---

Bad: "The teacher speaks with a mysterious smile, in a voice carrying centuries of wisdom:"

Good: "TEACHER: 'You already know.'"

---

Bad: "A vicious bloody battle ensues, in which the hero fights for his life, baring his resolve."

Good: "HERO STRIKES. ENEMY FALLS. HERO TURNS. SECOND ENEMY ALREADY BEHIND HIM."

## CHARACTER VOICE (SPEECH IDENTITY)

Every main character must have a **recognisable voice**. Read a line without the name attached — it should be obvious who is speaking.

Voice parameters:
- **Line length** — short / long
- **Register** — high / low, literary / colloquial
- **Slang** — yes / no, what kind
- **Self-evaluation in speech** — does this character justify themselves, admit fault, deflect
- **Silence** — who stays silent more than the others? Silence is a characterisation.
- **Patterns** — what phrases recur for this character?

A strong device: give each character a **unique form of address** for the same other character. One uses the name. Another uses a nickname. A third never addresses them directly.

---

## RHYTHM AND TEMPO

- **Fight / chase** — short verb-driven lines. One verb per line. The rhythm of cutting.

  ```
  HERO STRIKES.
  ENEMY FALLS.
  HERO RAISES the sword.
  ```

- **Dialogue** — short lines, pauses through action ("HERO is silent," not "the hero says nothing because he doesn't know what to say").

- **Description scenes** — also through action, never through state.

  Bad: "The atmosphere grew increasingly tense."
  Good: "The light dies. Only breathing."

---

## AVOID

- **Internal monologues.** If you can show through action, show.
- **Landscape descriptions longer than one line.** One frame, one line.
- **Mood adjectives** ("anxious," "ominous," "mysterious"). That's the audience's mood, not the screenwriter's.
- **Direct exposition through dialogue** ("As you remember, we are close relatives, and our mother was killed in 1992"). This is "info dump" — failure.
- **Lines that repeat what is already on screen.** If the hero is crying, no need for "I'm sad."

---

## SUPPORT

- **Objects as mines.** Every object's appearance should drive the plot. If you show a gun in act one, it must fire in act three (Chekhov's rule). If you show it twice, you must use it the third time.

- **Repeated lines.** The same line spoken in different contexts becomes a thematic mine. "I love you" early in the film and "I love you" before a murder are not the same line.

- **Silence.** A character's silence in a moment when the audience expects a line is the loudest sound in the film.

- **Symmetry.** The first scene and the last scene should rhyme — through an object, a location, a line, or an action.

---

## DIALOGUE FOR SHORT-FORM (15-90s)

Most Shorts must work on mute. When VO or dialogue is used:

- Use dialogue only to reveal what **cannot** be shown visually.
- One line per scene maximum.
- Start mid-thought. Hide exposition in action and emotion.
- Flag any line that is pure exposition dump and find the character-driven version.

For on-screen text cards:
- 5-10 words max per card.
- Text should ADD to the visual, not describe it.
- 1.5-2.5 seconds per card on screen.

---

## BILINGUAL SCRIPTS

When the project is bilingual (e.g. dialogue in English, Russian translation in parens beneath, or vice versa):

- Slug-lines are **always one language** (the production language).
- Action lines are **one language** (the production language).
- Only **dialogue** is bilingual: primary line in main language, translation in parens directly underneath.
- The translation does not add screen time. Count only the primary language for runtime.

Example layout:

```
HERO
We've waited long enough.
(Мы ждали достаточно долго.)
```

## FORMAT PER PROJECT TYPE

| Project | Style of action lines | Average scene length |
|---|---|---|
| Feature | Tight, prose-rhythm OK in slow scenes | 1-3 pages |
| TV pilot | Faster, more dialogue density | 1-2 pages |
| 90s Short | Full three-act, compressed prose | 1-1.5 pages total |
| 60s Short | Compressed, beat-per-line | <1 page total |
| 30s Short | Compact format, beat list | half page |
| 15s Short | Beat list only — visual + text + audio per beat | quarter page |

Short-form formats are detailed in `short-form.md`.
