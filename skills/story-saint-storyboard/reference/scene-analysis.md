# Scene Analysis: Dramatic → Visual Translation

## The 7-Question Method

This is the engine that converts story intent into camera decisions. Without this step, storyboarding becomes arbitrary framing with no narrative purpose.

### Question 1: What is the PURPOSE of this scene?

Every scene exists for a reason in the larger story. Common purposes:
- Introduce a character or relationship
- Reveal information (to character, audience, or both)
- Escalate conflict
- Show a decision being made
- Create suspense or dread
- Provide emotional release
- Transition between story phases

**Visual implication:** Purpose determines PACING. An introduction is slower (wider shots, held longer). A reveal is faster (tighter shots, quicker cuts). A decision scene oscillates (CU → WS → CU as the character weighs options).

### Question 2: What does the MAIN CHARACTER WANT?

The character's desire in the scene determines whose POV we favor. The camera should spend more time close to the character who wants something — their reactions matter most.

**Visual implication:** The wanting character gets more CUs, more favorable angles, and more screen time. The other characters are often seen in relation to the wanting character (OTS, POV).

### Question 3: What does the character SEE/EXPERIENCE?

What is the character's sensory world? What are they physically encountering?

**Visual implication:** This directly generates shots. If she sees a long empty hallway — we need that shot. If he hears footsteps behind him — we need the reaction CU before the reveal. Every sense impression in the script can become a shot.

### Question 4: What does the character EXPECT?

Expectation creates setup. The audience needs to understand what the character thinks will happen so the actual outcome has impact.

**Visual implication:** Setup shots should feel "normal" for the expected outcome. If the character expects a friendly meeting, use welcoming framing (eye-level, warm, medium distance). This makes the disruption more powerful.

### Question 5: What ACTUALLY happens?

The gap between expectation and reality is the scene's dramatic engine.

**Visual implication:** The TURN — the moment reality diverges from expectation — should be marked by a visual shift. Options:
- Jump in shot size (MS → ECU)
- Angle change (eye level → low or high)
- New line of action (camera crosses to other side)
- Depth shift (background element becomes foreground)
- Lens change (normal → telephoto compression)

### Question 6: What should the AUDIENCE learn, and WHEN?

This is information control — the most powerful storytelling tool in editing.

Three modes:
- **Audience knows BEFORE character** = SUSPENSE (Hitchcock's bomb under the table)
- **Audience learns WITH character** = SURPRISE (the reveal hits both at once)
- **Audience learns AFTER character** = MYSTERY (character knows something we don't)

**Visual implication:**
- Suspense: Show the threat in a wide shot before the character enters the danger zone. Use A-before-Q editing.
- Surprise: Keep the reveal offscreen until the character discovers it. Tight framing, sudden cut.
- Mystery: Show the character's reaction without showing what caused it. CU of face, withhold the object.

### Question 7: What should the audience FEEL?

This is the emotional design of the scene. Emotion maps to physical camera properties:

| Desired Emotion | Shot Size | Angle | Movement | Lens |
|----------------|-----------|-------|----------|------|
| Intimacy | CU / ECU | Eye level | Minimal, steady | Normal-telephoto |
| Anxiety | MCU-MS | Slightly low or high | Slight unsteadiness | Normal |
| Dread | WS showing emptiness | High or extreme low | Slow, deliberate dolly | Wide |
| Excitement | Varies rapidly | Varied, dynamic | Fast cuts, tracking | Wide-normal |
| Sadness | MS-CU | Eye level or slightly high | Minimal, held | Normal-telephoto |
| Awe | ELS / WS | Low angle | Slow crane up or tilt | Wide |
| Claustrophobia | MCU-CU | Flat, tight framing | Static or micro-movements | Telephoto (compressed) |
| Detachment | WS-ELS | High angle | Static, observational | Normal-telephoto |

## Scene Type Deep Dives

### DIALOGUE-STATIC Scenes
The bread and butter. Characters in a fixed location talking.

Key decisions:
1. Where in the room/space are they? (This determines the circle of dramatic action)
2. Are they equals or is one dominant? (Determines height, angle, shot size balance)
3. Does the power dynamic shift during the scene? (If yes, staging must shift too)
4. How intimate is the conversation? (Determines base shot size — a whispered secret ≠ a business meeting)

### DIALOGUE-MOBILE Scenes
Characters talk while moving (walk-and-talk, pacing, following someone).

Key decisions:
1. Does the movement have a destination? (If yes, the movement itself has narrative — arrival = resolution)
2. Who leads the movement? (Camera tends to favor/follow the leader)
3. Is the movement agitated or purposeful? (Agitated = handheld/unsteady, purposeful = smooth tracking)

### REVEAL Scenes
Information is disclosed.

Structure:
- **Pre-reveal:** Establish normal expectation
- **The reveal moment:** Maximum visual emphasis (size change, angle change, or both)
- **Post-reveal reaction:** CU of the character processing

The reveal is the single most important shot in the scene. Everything else is setup or reaction.

### ESTABLISHING Scenes
Introduce a location, time, or mood.

Three strategies (from shot-flow-and-editing.md):
1. **Wide → Tight:** Classic. Show the whole, then move into the detail.
2. **Tight → Wide:** Show a mysterious detail first, then pull back to context.
3. **Detail montage:** Multiple CUs that let the audience construct the whole.

Strategy 3 is the most narratively engaging because the audience does the work of assembling meaning.

## Common Failure Modes in Scene Analysis

| Failure | Symptom | Fix |
|---------|---------|-----|
| No clear turn identified | Every shot feels the same size/angle | Re-read the scene — find the moment something changes. If nothing changes, the scene may not be needed. |
| All shots favor one character | The other characters feel like props | Add reaction shots. The listener is as important as the speaker. |
| Information dump | Wall of dialogue, no visual variation | Break the dialogue with visual business (character does something while talking) and shift framing to maintain interest. |
| Pretty but empty shots | Great compositions with no narrative purpose | Ask: what Q does this shot raise? What A does it give? If neither — cut it. |
| Obvious shot choices | Master → OTS → OTS → CU, repeat | This is coverage, not visualization. What makes THIS scene different from every other conversation? Find the unique visual idea. |
