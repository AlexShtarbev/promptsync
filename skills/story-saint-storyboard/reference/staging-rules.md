# Staging Rules: Patterns, Positions, Camera Placement

## The Line of Action (180° Rule)

### Setup
1. Identify the two characters currently in dialogue
2. Draw an imaginary line between their eyes (the sight line)
3. Choose ONE SIDE of that line for the camera
4. All setups for this dialogue exchange stay on that side

### Crossing the Line (When Permitted)
- A character physically moves to the other side → new line established
- The camera dollies/cranes across in one continuous move
- A cutaway to a neutral detail (clock, hands, object) → reestablish on the other side
- A new character enters and a new sight line becomes dominant

### For Moving Subjects
The line follows the dominant direction of motion (not sight lines). Cars chasing = line is the road. But if drivers look at each other, a temporary sight line overrides the motion line for those shots.

## The Triangle System: 5 Setups from 3 Positions

For two characters (A and B) with the line between them:

```
Position 1 (Wing Left)     Position 2 (Center)     Position 3 (Wing Right)
   ↓                            ↓                        ↓
Angular on A              Master Two-Shot           Angular on B
OTS favoring B            (both in frame)           OTS favoring A
POV-A (seeing B)                                    POV-B (seeing A)
Profile A                                           Profile B
```

Any shot from any position cuts with any other. This is the safety net that guarantees coverage. But your storyboard should be more specific than the triangle — it should have a REASON for each shot choice.

## The 10 Two-Player Positions

### Decision Matrix: Which Position for Which Scene?

| Scene Situation | Best Position(s) | Reason |
|----------------|-------------------|--------|
| Direct confrontation / argument | P1 (face-to-face) | Maximum opposition |
| Job interview / interrogation | P1 or P6 (in-depth) | Formal, evaluative |
| Two people working on shared task | P2 (shoulder-to-shoulder) | Alliance, co-operation |
| Casual early-relationship chat | P3 (90-degree) | Non-committal, relaxed |
| One character hiding emotion | P4 (one turned away) | Viewer sees what other can't |
| Emotional standoff / shutdown | P5 (both away) | Maximum separation |
| Confession / monologue to unresponsive listener | P6 (same direction, depth) | Speaker isolated, listener withheld |
| Distracted, divided attention scene | P7 (both offscreen-look) | Relaxed, indirect |
| Covert observation scene | P8 (right angle, BG away) | Unequal awareness |
| Comedy standoff or surveillance | P9 (backs to each other) | Stylized tension |
| Power differential (seated/standing) | P10 (different heights) | Vertical dominance |

### Position Details

**P1: Face-to-face (shoulders parallel)**
- Profile two-shot: powerful opposition, limited expression visibility
- OTS pairs: the classic shot/reverse shot — most versatile, most options
- Tight OTS: block 1/3 to 1/2 of frame with foreground head/shoulder
- Long lens OTS (120-200mm): intimate, compressed
- Low-angle reverses (over-the-hip): adversarial, dynamic, camera at ~3ft

**P2: Shoulder-to-shoulder (both facing same way)**
- Frontal two-shot: both visible, can turn in profile to each other
- Profile CUs: camera stays on same angle axis as the two-shot
- Angular CUs: camera moved to side, stronger sense of presence

**P3: 90-degree angle**
- Angular two-shot: one player favored (faces camera more)
- Head-on two-shot: both equal — good master
- Tight two-shot with closed space: maintains the angular feel

**P4: One turned away (depth staging)**
- Wide lens (30mm) foreground = slight distortion, BG feels far
- CU of hidden face: privileged viewer position
- No reverses used typically — this is inherently frontal

**P5: Both facing away**
- High angle opener: tension, isolation
- Reversed angular shots (crossing the line): deliberate rule-break for dramatic purpose
- Body language dominates: folded arms, hands in pockets

**P6: In-depth, same direction**
- Wide shot: both visible, eye contact withheld
- MS of foreground: identification with this character
- BG player can move, pace, turn — FG player restricted
- Over-cutting destroys this staging's unity

**P7-P9: Indirect positions**
- All rely on the HEAD TURN as the key dramatic action
- Head turn = aural "cut on the look" — character responding to offscreen sound/speech
- Tighter framings make these less stylized, more natural

**P10: Different heights**
- Tight two-shots become up/down angles naturally
- Wide framings can show height without forced angles
- Combine with P1-P9 for compound positions

## Three-Player Staging

### Pattern Recognition

```
Are 2 characters on one side, 1 on the other?
├─ Is the lone character BETWEEN the other two?
│   └─ YES → A Pattern (flanked)
├─ Is the lone character to ONE SIDE of the other two?
│   └─ YES → L Pattern (isolated side)
└─ Are all three in a line?
    └─ YES → I Pattern (linear)
```

### Camera Placement for Three Players

**A Pattern:**
- The three-shot with all visible = natural master shot
- OTS shots unify space better than isolated singles
- Line of action shifts as dialogue shifts between pairs
- Reuse camera positions rather than creating new ones for each line shift

**L Pattern:**
- The two players on the long side form a natural two-shot
- The single player on the short side addresses the pair
- L pattern naturally groups two players as a unit vs. one

**Key rule:** Any three-player scene reduces to I-pattern pairs for close-ups and camera placement. The patterns just describe the overall arrangement.

## Four+ Players

### Simplification Strategy
1. Identify the 2-3 characters who DRIVE the dialogue
2. Establish the line between them
3. Remaining characters = background/reaction shots
4. Use NO MORE than 4 sight lines total
5. Establish a general viewing direction — don't ping-pong the camera

### Circular Seating (Dinner Table, Meeting)
- Pick one character as the anchor viewpoint (often the most reactive character, not the speaker)
- Shoot most coverage over their shoulder
- Only one reverse needed to see the anchor
- Multiple sight lines are available — use the most dramatically relevant pair at any moment

## Mobile Staging

### The Core Trade
Actor movement can substitute for cutting. If an actor turns around, you get a "reverse shot" without moving the camera.

### Counter Move
Camera moves one direction, actor moves the opposite. Covers multiple framings in one take:
- Start: OTS wide
- Middle: actor crosses, camera dollies forward
- End: new position — MS or CU of a different character

### Building Sequence Shots from Static Positions
Take the positions from the static staging system and connect them with movement:
1. Start in Position X
2. Actor moves → transition
3. Arrive at Position Y
4. Continue dialogue in new framing

Each "waypoint" is a position from the 10-position system. The movement between them is the choreography.

### When to Use Mobile vs. Cut
- **Mobile staging:** When continuous time and spatial unity matter. When movement itself is dramatic (restlessness, pursuit, arrival).
- **Cutting:** When you need to compare reactions, control information flow, create rhythm through juxtaposition, or shift time.

## Solo Character Staging

When only one character is on screen:

### Shot Size Progression
- Establish context (WS showing character in environment)
- Move to emotional engagement (MS-CU showing expression/action)
- Punctuate with details (ECU of hands, objects, relevant environment)

### In the Action vs. Outside
- **Camera inside the dramatic circle:** We share the character's space. More intimate, more identification.
- **Camera outside:** We observe. More detached, more ironic or ominous.

### Depth Composition for Solo
Place meaningful objects at different depths:
- Foreground: Something commenting on the character (a clock = time running out)
- Middleground: The character
- Background: The environment telling us something (empty room = isolation)

## Action Scene Staging

### Line of Action for Movement
The line follows the dominant motion path. All camera positions stay on one side of this path to maintain consistent screen direction.

### Key Rule
Crossing the line reverses screen direction. This can be used deliberately for disorientation (fight scenes) but confuses geography if done accidentally.

### Cutting Strategy for Action
- Cut on the action (mid-movement hides the edit)
- Exits and entrances: cut while subject is partially in frame
- Clear frame: let subject exit completely, hold empty frame, cut to new shot
